\section{Proposed Design of a 360$\degree$ \gls{lidar} Platform}
\label{sec:proposed_design}
Based on the analysis of existing DIY solutions for 360° \gls{lidar} platforms, the proposed design for this miniproject involves using a 2D \gls{lidar} sensor mounted on a tilted platform that is able to rotate around its axis.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \caption{Proposed platform design. \protect$\theta$ is the tilt angle}
    \input{design/proposed_design/tikz_platform.tex}
    \label{fig:tikz_platform}
\end{figure}
This design leverages the advantages of 2D \gls{lidar} sensors, such as fast data acquisition and high resolution, while also eliminating some of the drawbacks of previous designs, namely:
\begin{itemize}[label=\textcolor{green!60!black}{\textbf{+}}]
    \item The platform will be pretty rigid, minimising vibrations
    \item Mechanical design is very simple, making it easy to build and troubleshoot
    \item The resulting point cloud will be more uniformly distributed compared to the one obtained using the tilting platform design presented in \cref{sec:tilting_platform} unless the tilt angle is extremely steep (see \cref{fig:rotate_pointCloud})
\end{itemize}

Such a design does have its drawbacks, however. The first one is immediately apparent from \cref{fig:rotate_pointCloud}: there are blind spots in the point cloud where no points are measured.
While these blind spots can be minimised by choosing a large tilt angle, this approach reintroduces the problem of non-uniform point cloud density.

Besides that, compared to the tilting platform design presented in \cref{sec:tilting_platform}, the proposed design would need to rotate across twice as large a range to obtain full coverage of the environment, 
since the tilting platform scanned only within $[-90\degree, +90\degree]$, whereas the current design must complete a full 360-degree rotation. This would lead to longer scanning times.

\subsection{Controlling the Platform}
Since we want our platform to be precisely controlled, we need to choose an appropriate motor.
Stepper motors are commonly used in applications requiring precise positioning, as they can be controlled to move in small, discrete steps.
For this design, a NEMA 17 17HS8401 stepper motor is used to rotate the \gls{lidar} platform.

For heavy load applications, it is important to consider the potential error sources of stepper motors, such as the stepper motor losing steps due to a heavy load or by driving the motor faster than its maximum speed \cite[p.~13]{friedl_SpeedControlStepper_2008}.
However, in our case, the load will be relatively light for this motor, and the speed will be kept low; thus, a feedback mechanism for correcting lost steps is not required.
\subsection{Choosing the Tilt Angle}
The tilt angle of the \gls{lidar} platform is a crucial parameter that affects the size of the aforementioned blind spots in the point cloud (see \cref{fig:rotate_pointCloud}).
However, increasing the tilt angle also increases the spacing between the scans when the platform is rotated in discrete steps (see \cref{fig:scan_spacing}). 
\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    
    \begin{subfigure}{0.45\linewidth}
        \centering
        \input{design/proposed_design/scan_spacing.tex}
        \caption{3D simulation}
        \label{fig:scan_spacing:3d}
    \end{subfigure}
    \hfill
    \begin{subfigure}{0.45\linewidth}
        \centering
        \input{design/proposed_design/tikz_scan_spacing.tex}
        \caption{Simplified image showing the dependence of scan spacing $S_{\text{scan}}$ on tilt angle $\theta$}
        \label{fig:scan_spacing:tikz}
    \end{subfigure}

    \caption{Visualisation of scan spacing dependence on the tilt angle}
    \label{fig:scan_spacing}
\end{figure}
It is obvious from the \cref{fig:scan_spacing:tikz}, that
\begin{equation}
    S_{\text{scan}} = \varphi_{\text{step}}\cdot\sin(\theta),
\end{equation}
where the step angle $\varphi_{\text{step}}$ is constant and is equal to 1.8$\degree$ for our motor (see \cref{tab:stepper_motor_params}).
The following graph shows how the spacing changes with the tilt angle, providing our \gls{lidar}'s resolution as a reference value, which we would like to achieve by choosing a correct tilt angle:
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \input{design/proposed_design/angleRes_tiltOnly.tex}
    \caption{Scan spacing depending on the tilt angle $\theta$}
    \label{fig:angleRes_tiltOnly}
\end{figure}    
As could be seen from \cref{fig:angleRes_tiltOnly}, the spacing will be the same as the angular resolution of the \gls{lidar} itself at the tilt angle of approximately $\si{23.4}{\degree}$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \input{design/proposed_design/23point4rotate_pointCloud.tex}
    \caption{Side profile of a point cloud obtained using a rotating tilted platform with tilt angle $\theta=23.4\degree$}
    \label{fig:23point4rotate_pointCloud}
\end{figure}
Examining the side profile of the point cloud obtained using this tilt angle, we can see that the blind spots are extremely large, making this tilt angle impractical for our application.
Thankfully, there is a way to achieve a more complete coverage of the environment without sacrificing point cloud density.
\subsection{Choosing a Gear Ratio for the Rotating Platform}
To eliminate the need for a compromise between point cloud coverage and density when using a stepper motor to rotate the platform, we can incorporate a gear mechanism into our design.
Using gears with a specific gear ratio allows us to choose a tilt angle that minimises blind spots while still producing a dense point cloud.
The resulting spacing between scans can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
    S_{\text{scan}} = R_{\text{gear}}\cdot\varphi_{\text{step}}\cdot\sin(\theta),
\end{equation}
where $R_{\text{gear}}$ is the ratio of the number of teeth on the driven gear to the number of teeth on the driving gear. A following graph is similar to \cref{fig:angleRes_tiltOnly}, but the third dimension represents different gear ratios:
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{design/proposed_design/angleRes_TiltPlusGearbox.png}
    \caption{Point cloud resolution depending on the tilt angle $\theta$ and gear ratio}
    \label{ph:angleRes_tiltPlusGearbox}
\end{figure}

As can be seen from \cref{ph:angleRes_tiltPlusGearbox}, using a gearbox allows us to choose from a wide range of tilt angles without compromising on the spacing between scans.
\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Best combinations for step angle/gear ratio/tilt angle}
    \rowcolors{2}{ctubluebg!20}{ctubluebg!65}
    \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|}
        \toprule
        Step Angle [$\degree$] & Gear Ratio [-] & Tilt Angle [$\degree$] \\
        \midrule
        1,2 & 3:2 & 37 \\
        1,0 & 9:5 & 46 \\
        0,9 & 2:1 & 53 \\
        0,8 & 9:4 & 64 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \label{tab:best_combinations}
\end{table}

Of course, by using the gearbox, we increase the number of steps required for a complete revolution, thereby increasing the scanning time.
Nevertheless, by providing platforms of different sizes with appropriate gear ratios, we can create a modular platform that can be reassembled depending on the application.
